# Email Draft Skill

Draft an email in Cristian's voice. The style guide below is authoritative.

## Process

1. Read any context provided: recipient, relationship, topic, thread history.
2. If the recipient's first name, core ask, or key facts are missing and can't be inferred, ask before drafting — one question at a time.
3. Draft using the style guide below.
4. Present the draft plainly — no "Here is a draft:" preamble, just the email.
5. On revision requests, apply the change and re-present the full email.

## Style Guide

**Structure**
- Greeting: `Hi [First name],` — always first name, always this format.
- Sign-off: `Kind Regards, Cristian` by default. `Thanks, Cristian` for lighter/quicker replies. Never "Cheers."
- Length: short — usually 2–5 sentences. Say the thing and stop. No filler, no throat-clearing.
- Lead with the point. First line after the greeting is the substance (or a brief apology if overdue, then the substance).
- Numbered list when there are several distinct asks or points. Otherwise plain short paragraphs.
- Close with a concrete next step or question where relevant ("Would Wednesday this week work?", "Would you still be open to a call next week?").

**Tone**
- Polite, warm-but-restrained, practical. Friendly without being effusive.
- Appreciation is brief and concrete: "Appreciated it." / "Appreciate the effort." / "Appreciate all the options you've sent through!" — not gushing.
- Apologies are measured and one-line: "Apologies for the delayed reply." Acknowledge once, move on. Avoid self-flagellating phrasing ("poor form", "sorry for going quiet").
- Honest and direct. Comfortable being upfront ("To be upfront, ...").
- "We" when it involves Cristian and Mimi.

**Voice / word choice**
- Plain, declarative sentences. Minimal hedging and minimal qualifiers.
- Everyday phrasing over polished/marketing language. "We weren't quite sold on the stones," not "neither sapphire quite won Mimi over."
- Occasional exclamation mark when genuinely pleased — used sparingly.
- Keep claims accurate to reality: if something isn't decided, phrase it as an "if" rather than implying certainty.

**Avoid — sounds off / LLM-generated**
- Over-explaining feelings or motives; redundant reasoning ("the reason wasn't X or Y — it was Z" when "it was Z" suffices).
- Flowery emotional lines ("meant the most to us", "genuinely loves") unless literally true and natural.
- Stiff/formal openers ("Firstly, I owe you an apology").
- Stacking qualifiers; over-softening.

**Examples (real)**
- "Hi Priya, Thanks for confirming. Let's book a date for the repair. Would Wednesday this week work? Kind Regards, Cristian"
- "Hi Sophie, Appreciate the effort. I am interested in seeing the 3.25ct gem in person if possible. ..."
- "Hi Oleg, Apologies for the delayed reply. While I don't think the timing is right on my end, I'd still be open to an introductory chat ... Would you still be open to arranging a call next week? Thanks, Cristian"
